Evaluating Relative Localization in GPS-Denied Environments Using Starling2 UAVs

Authors: Taiwo Hazeez, Cosme Penney, Daniel Guido, Pratik Mukherjee

This project evaluates the use of AprilTags and onboard cameras for relative localization between drones operating in GPS-denied environments. Experiments were conducted at multiple distances and benchmarked against OptiTrack motion capture data. We also tested a YOLOv8 neural network for drone detection.

To enable accurate pose estimation between UAVs in GPS-denied environments, we rely on a combination of visual odometry and inertial sensing. Our system uses AprilTags attached to one drone and onboard cameras on the other for relative localization. This allows us to benchmark how well the drone can detect and estimate position in real-time without external localization infrastructure.

System Overview

We conducted experiments in a controlled indoor environment equipped with 12 OptiTrack motion capture cameras. This system provides high-accuracy ground truth pose data, which we use as a benchmark to evaluate the onboard relative localization performance. Each drone was tagged with reflective markers to allow simultaneous tracking via OptiTrack while also performing onboard AprilTag detection.

Experimental Setup

Our experimental results demonstrate a clear relationship between inter-agent distance and localization accuracy. At shorter ranges (3 ft and 6 ft), the AprilTag-based detection was stable and showed only minor positional error when compared to OptiTrack. At the furthest distance of 12 ft, detection reliability and positional precision decreased due to resolution and field-of-view limitations.
